Codon64

Encoder & Decoder

Explanation

There are 4 nucleotides in DNA: guanine (G), adenine (A), cytosine (C), and thymine (T). We can assign a numerical representation to each base. Such as A=0, C=1, G=2, and T=3. Then nucleotides can be thought of as a Base4 system.

Codons are made of nucleotide triplets, which means there are 64 total codons (4x4x4). Thus, codons as a whole can be thought of as a Base64 system. Wherein each codon is capable of encoding 6 bits. Similar to how each symbol in Base64 encodes 6 bits.

This is the basis for Codon64. Just like in Base64, we map each 6 bits of input to an output symbol. The main differences is that in Codon64, the symbols are codons, which are expressed with three characters instead of one. Technically, it's also valid to think of this as a Base4 system, in which A, C, G, and T are the 4 digit instead of 0, 1, 2, and 3.

And just like in Base64, equal signs are used for padding.
